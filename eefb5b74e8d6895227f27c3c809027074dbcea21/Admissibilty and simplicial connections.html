Motivation for definitions of admissibility

As a first approximation to an explanation, we consider the category  whose objects are pairs (V,φ) of finite-dimensional vector spaces along with an endomorphism, and the morphisms f:(V,φ)→(W,ψ) are the morphisms f:V→W of vector spaces such that the obvious square commutes (i.e. such that f∘φ=ψ∘f). We are constructing the toy example, where we replace vector bundles by vector spaces, and curvatures of connections by endomorphisms: we say that (V,φ) is flat if φ=0.

  • Firstly, we consider the Grothendieck group K(), whose objects are isomorphism classes of objects of , and where, for each short exact sequence 0→A→B→C→0 in  we introduce the relation [A]−[B]+[C]=0, whence [(V,φ)]+[(W,ψ)]:=(V⊕W,φ⊕ψ).
    Then we introduce the relation [(V,0)]∼[(0,0)] on K() to obtain K()/∼.

  • Then, consider the endofunctor E:→ that sends (V,φ) to (V/Kerφ,φ). Write LE to mean the localisation of  at all morphisms that become isomorphisms after applying E, the set of which we call .

  • Lemma. A morphism is in  if and only if it is admissible, i.e. if and only if there exist decompositions V≅V1⊕V2 and W≅W1⊕W2 such that φ(V1)=0 and ψ(W1)=0, and f:V→W descends to an isomorphism V/V1−→∼W/W1.

    Proof. Let f:(V,φ)→(W,ψ) be a morphism in , i.e. such that E(f) is an isomorphism. Then we take V1:=Kerφ and W1:=Kerψ.

    Conversely, let f:(V,φ)→(W,ψ) be admissible. Then V1⩽Kerφ, and so, by the third isomorphism theorem, (V/V1)/(Kerφ/V1)≅V/Kerφ. It remains then to show that E(f) restricts to an isomorphism Kerφ/V1−→∼Kerψ/W1. But f∘φ=ψ∘f, and so E(f)∘E(φ)∘E(f)−1=E(ψ), whence E(f):Kerφ/V1=KerE(φ)−→∼KerE(ψ)=Kerψ/W1.

  • Lemma. There is a (canonical) isomorphism K(LE)→K()/∼.

    Proof. It suffices to take the 'identity' map, as follows. Take two isomorphic objects (V,φ),(W,ψ)∈LE. Then, either the isomorphism between them is given by an isomorphism in , or by some morphism in . In the former case, we are done; in the latter case, the above lemma tells us that there is an admissible decomposition V≅V1⊕V2→W1⊕W2≅W. But then both (V2,φ) and (W2,ψ) are equivalent to zero, whence f:(V1,φ)−→∼(W1,ψ) gives us an isomorphism in K()/∼.

    Conversely, take two isomorphic objects (V,φ),(W,ψ)∈. Then they are also isomorphic in LE (since all isomorphisms are in ). Further, if [(V,φ)]∼[(0,0)] in K()/∼ then φ=0, whence V/Kerφ=0, and so [(V,φ)]=[(0,0)]∈K(LE).

  • Now assume that we have some invariant (i.e. under base-change) polynomial P from ⊗n to some (additive, say) abelian group G (usually taken to be ℂ). If P is additive then it will descend to a well-defined polynomial on K()⊗n. If P sends a tensor product of n flat objects to zero then it also descends to a well-defined polynomial on K()/∼. Thus, by the above lemma, we get P defined on K(LE). This means, by the above-above lemma, that our characteristic classes are invariant under an admissible connection, and thus, in the admissible simplicial connection setting, the curvatures will glue.

    There is some subtlety here though: the exponential Chern classes and the Chern character are both additive, but the usual Chern classes aren't. We can get around this, however, by not using the Grothendieck group K() in the above, but instead the 'smaller' version, where we say that E+F=(E⊕F) only when at least one of E and F are flat.

Admissible simplicial forms

  • Definition. An endomorphism-valued simplicial form is said to be admissible if it is fibrewise admissible. That is, the morphism

    ∙fip:(Y∙fip)∗p−1→p
    of sheaves on Yp induces a morphism
    (Y∙fip×id)∗(p−1⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯,ωp−1)→(id×fip)∗(p⎯⎯⎯⎯,ωp).

    Fibrewise, this gives us a family of morphisms of pairs of vector spaces and endomorphisms, since, over a point in the base space Yp×Δp−1,

    1. the simplicial bundle p⎯⎯⎯⎯ looks like ℂr(p) (see below); and
    2. the endomorphism-valued simplicial form gives us a family of endomorphisms, indexed by tangent vectors.

    For ω∙ to be admissible then, we want, for all choices of tangent vectors, the induced morphism of pairs of vector spaces and endomorphisms to be admissible.

    N.B. There are no conditions on the ranks r(p) of each simplicial level of our bundle, and so the rank may well jump. In practice, however, we often have a tamer situation, as described below.

  • Criterion. For an endomorphism-valued simplicial form ω∙ to be an admissible, it is sufficient to ask for sub-bundles p,p↪p that lie in the kernel of the endomorphisms and such that the morphism

    (Y∙fip)∗p−1→p
    maps one to the other, i.e. induces a map
    (Y∙fip)∗p−1→p.

Admissible simplicial connections

  • Definition. We say that a simplicial connection is admissible if its curvature is an admissible endomorphism-valued simplicial 2-form.
  • Criterion. For a simplicial connection ∇∙ to be admissible, it is sufficient to ask for splittings
    p−1⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯≅p−1⊕p−1p⎯⎯⎯⎯≅p⊕p−1

    such that p−1 is ∇p−1-flat, p is ∇p-flat, and the morphism
    (Y∙fip×id)∗(p−1⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯,∇p−1)→(id×fip)∗(p⎯⎯⎯⎯,∇p).

    restricts to a map p−1↪p, and the induced map
    p−1⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯/p−1→p⎯⎯⎯⎯/p
    is an isomorphism.

  • N.B. The difference of two admissible simplicial connections has no reason a priori to be an admissible endomorphism-valued simplicial 1-form, because the splittings might be different. If, however, they agree, then their difference will indeed be an admissible form. This prompts the following definition.
  • Definition. A family of admissible simplicial connections is said to be compatible if they all share the same splittings.
  • Slogan. Admissible connections give us simplicial curvature; compatible families of admissible connections let us transgress.
